How to Read the Whole Bible in a Year (Without Giving Up in February)

Almost everyone who starts a Bible-in-a-year plan means it. Far fewer finish. The difference is rarely willpower — it's the plan. Here's how to set one up so you actually make it to Revelation.

Every January, thousands of people open to Genesis 1 with real resolve. By mid-February a large share have quietly stopped — not because they stopped caring, but because a few missed days piled into a backlog that felt impossible to clear. The good news: finishing the whole Bible in a year is very achievable, and it has far more to do with how you structure the plan than with how disciplined you are. Let's walk through it.

First, the math: how much is "the whole Bible in a year"?

The Protestant Bible has 1,189 chapters. Spread evenly across 365 days, that's about 3.3 chapters a day — roughly 12 to 15 minutes of reading for most people. That number surprises people, because "read the entire Bible" sounds enormous while "read three chapters" sounds like nothing. Both are true. The whole project fits into the gap between your alarm and your coffee.

A few reference points as you plan your pace:

  • 1 year — about 3–4 chapters a day. The classic, gentle pace.
  • 6 months — about 7 chapters a day. Noticeably faster, still comfortable.
  • 90 days — about 13 chapters a day. An intense "big picture" read that many people love doing once.

Step 1: Pick a plan structure that fits how you read

There is no single "correct" Bible reading plan. There are a few common structures, and the best one is the one you'll actually keep. The main options:

  • Front to back (canonical). Genesis to Revelation, in order. Simple and satisfying — you always know exactly where you are. The one hazard is the stretch of Old Testament law in Leviticus and Numbers, where many readers stall.
  • Old and New Testament together. A couple of chapters from the Old Testament and one from the New each day. You get narrative variety and never go months without reaching the Gospels. A great choice for first-time finishers.
  • Chronological. Passages arranged in the rough order the events happened, so the storyline flows. Wonderful for seeing the big picture, though the order can feel unfamiliar.
  • Gospels or New Testament only. If reading the whole Bible feels like too much for this season, start smaller. The New Testament is 260 chapters — under a chapter a day finishes it in a year with room to spare.

If you've never finished before, the "Old and New Testament together" approach tends to have the highest success rate, simply because there's always something moving forward.

Step 2: Choose a realistic pace — and build in margin

This is the step that quietly decides whether you finish. Most plans assume a perfect 365-day streak, which no ordinary life sustains. Sick days, travel, and hard weeks are not failures of character; they're a certainty. So plan for them from the start.

The simplest fix is to read six days a week instead of seven, leaving one day open to rest or catch up. You still finish comfortably inside the year, but now a missed Tuesday has somewhere to go. That single change is the biggest difference between plans that get finished and plans that get abandoned.

Consistency over a year matters far more than a perfect record in January.

Step 3: Anchor the reading to something you already do

Willpower is unreliable; habits are not. Instead of "I'll read the Bible sometime today," attach the reading to a fixed anchor already in your day — right after you pour your morning coffee, on the train, or before you turn off the light at night. When the new habit rides on an old one, you stop having to decide, and deciding is the part that fails.

Keep your Bible (or app) physically where that anchor happens. A Bible left open on the kitchen table the night before gets read far more often than one on a shelf in another room.

Step 4: Make your progress visible

A plan you can see is a plan you keep. There's a small but real motivation in ticking off a box each day and watching the year fill in. It turns an abstract goal into a physical streak you don't want to break — the same reason habit trackers work.

This is exactly what a printed plan gives you that a reading app often doesn't: the whole journey on one page, dated, with a checkbox for every day. Keep it tucked in your Bible or stuck to the fridge, and your progress is impossible to ignore.

What to do when you fall behind (because you will)

Here is the single most important rule, and it runs against instinct: when you fall behind, do not start over. Starting over from Genesis is the most common way a Bible-in-a-year attempt dies. It throws away real progress and rebuilds the exact backlog that discouraged you in the first place.

Instead, just pick up on today's date and keep moving. If you're a week behind, use your rest days to close part of the gap, or simply accept finishing a week or two into the next January. Finishing slightly late is still finishing — and it's infinitely better than the alternative of quitting to preserve a tidy streak.

Which Bible translation should I use?

For a full read-through, choose readability over everything. A translation you find easy and enjoyable to read is one you'll keep opening; a difficult one becomes a reason to stop. Modern, readable versions like the NIV, ESV, CSB, or NLT are all excellent for this. Reading plans list passages by reference — for example, John 1–3 — so the same plan works with any translation you already own or read in an app.

A quick recap

  1. It's only about 3–4 chapters a day — around 15 minutes.
  2. Pick a structure you'll enjoy; "Old + New Testament together" is a safe first bet.
  3. Read six days a week so missed days have somewhere to go.
  4. Anchor it to an existing daily habit.
  5. Print the plan so your progress stays visible.
  6. When you fall behind, continue from today — never restart.

Do those six things and the whole Bible in a year stops being a resolution you hope to keep and becomes a quiet, daily rhythm you actually finish.

Frequently asked questions

How many chapters a day is the Bible in a year?

The Bible has 1,189 chapters, so a 365-day plan averages a little over three chapters a day — usually 12 to 15 minutes of reading.

Can a beginner really read the whole Bible in a year?

Yes. The daily amount is small, and the difference between finishing and quitting is almost always the structure of the plan — a realistic pace with margin, a visible tracker, and a rule never to restart after falling behind.

What's the best Bible reading plan to finish in a year?

For first-timers, a plan that mixes an Old Testament and a New Testament reading each day has the highest completion rate, because there's always a storyline moving forward. If that feels like too much, start with the New Testament alone.

What should I do if I miss several days?

Don't start over. Pick up on today's date and use your weekly rest day to catch up. Finishing a little late is still finishing.
