18 Comforting Bible Verses About Anxiety and Worry

When worry is loud, Scripture speaks a steadier word. These eighteen verses — grouped by what your heart may need — are among the passages Christians have returned to for centuries when anxiety presses in. Read them slowly, and let them do their quiet work.

Anxiety is one of the most human things there is, and the Bible never treats it as a failure of faith to be ashamed of. Again and again, Scripture meets worried people gently — "don't be afraid," "cast your burden on him," "come to me and rest." The verses below are gathered by theme so you can go straight to the ones that fit today. Sit with one or two rather than racing through all eighteen; a single verse, truly taken to heart, can carry you further than a page skimmed. (Verses are quoted from the World English Bible, a modern, public-domain translation.)

When God tells you not to be anxious

Some of the clearest words on worry are also invitations — not just commands to stop, but a place to put the worry instead.

"In nothing be anxious, but in everything, by prayer and petition with thanksgiving, let your requests be made known to God. And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your thoughts in Christ Jesus." — Philippians 4:6–7
"casting all your worries on him, because he cares for you." — 1 Peter 5:7
"Therefore don't be anxious for tomorrow, for tomorrow will be anxious for itself. Each day's own evil is sufficient." — Matthew 6:34
"In the multitude of my thoughts within me, your comforts delight my soul." — Psalm 94:19

Notice that Philippians doesn't stop at "don't be anxious." It hands you something to do with the anxiety — pray, ask, give thanks — and promises a peace that stands guard over your mind. Worry always wants us to carry tomorrow today; these verses give us permission to set it down.

When you need to know God is with you

Much of our fear eases the moment we remember we are not facing it alone.

"Don't be afraid, for I am with you. Don't be dismayed, for I am your God. I will strengthen you. Yes, I will help you. Yes, I will uphold you with the right hand of my righteousness." — Isaiah 41:10
"Be strong and courageous. Don't be afraid. Don't be dismayed, for Yahweh your God is with you wherever you go." — Joshua 1:9
"Be strong and courageous… for Yahweh your God himself is who goes with you. He will not fail you nor forsake you." — Deuteronomy 31:6
"Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for you are with me." — Psalm 23:4

The command "don't be afraid" almost always comes attached to a reason: for I am with you. The point isn't to feel braver by willpower, but to remember Who is standing beside you in the thing you're dreading.

When you're looking for peace and rest

Anxiety is exhausting. These verses offer the rest that worry steals.

"Peace I leave with you. My peace I give to you; not as the world gives, I give to you. Don't let your heart be troubled, neither let it be fearful." — John 14:27
"Come to me, all you who labor and are heavily burdened, and I will give you rest… for my yoke is easy, and my burden is light." — Matthew 11:28–30
"You will keep whoever's mind is steadfast in perfect peace, because he trusts in you." — Isaiah 26:3
"Cast your burden on Yahweh and he will sustain you. He will never allow the righteous to be moved." — Psalm 55:22
"I have told you these things, that in me you may have peace. In the world you have trouble; but cheer up! I have overcome the world." — John 16:33

The peace Jesus offers is "not as the world gives" — not the fragile calm of everything going right, but a settledness that can hold even when things don't. Isaiah ties it to where the mind rests: a mind "steadfast" on God, rather than circling the worry, is kept in peace.

When fear strikes suddenly

For the moments when anxiety comes fast and physical, short verses are easiest to reach for.

"When I am afraid, I will put my trust in you." — Psalm 56:3
"I sought Yahweh, and he answered me, and delivered me from all my fears." — Psalm 34:4
"For God didn't give us a spirit of fear, but of power, love, and self-control." — 2 Timothy 1:7
"God is our refuge and strength, a very present help in trouble." — Psalm 46:1
"Don't be afraid, for I have redeemed you. I have called you by your name. You are mine." — Isaiah 43:1

Psalm 56:3 is honest — it doesn't say "I am never afraid," but "when I am afraid, I will put my trust in you." Fear and faith can occupy the same moment. The verse simply points the fear somewhere safe.

How to let these verses actually help

Reading a comforting verse once is good; keeping it near you is far better. When anxiety returns — and it does — you want the words already in reach, not somewhere in a search bar. Three simple, old practices make that happen:

  • Pray them back to God. Turn a verse into a prayer — "Lord, you said to cast my worries on you, so here is mine…" Writing your worries and the verse together on a prayer sheet keeps you focused and gives you something to look back on.
  • Memorize one or two. A short verse like Psalm 56:3 or 1 Peter 5:7, truly memorized, is there for you in the dark at 3 a.m. when no page is open.
  • Write them by hand. Copying a verse slowly settles it into memory and calms the mind in the doing — a small, steadying act of its own.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best Bible verse for anxiety?

Philippians 4:6–7 is the passage most often turned to, because it doesn't only say "don't be anxious" — it tells you what to do instead (pray, ask, give thanks) and promises the peace of God to guard your heart and mind. 1 Peter 5:7 and Psalm 56:3 are also loved for their simplicity.

What does the Bible say about worrying?

The Bible repeatedly and gently urges us not to be consumed by worry — "don't be anxious for tomorrow" (Matthew 6:34) — and points us to trust God with what we cannot control, casting our cares on him because he cares for us (1 Peter 5:7).

Are there short Bible verses about fear I can memorize?

Yes. Some of the shortest and most powerful are "When I am afraid, I will put my trust in you" (Psalm 56:3), "casting all your worries on him, because he cares for you" (1 Peter 5:7), and "God is our refuge and strength, a very present help in trouble" (Psalm 46:1).

Is it a sin to feel anxious?

Feeling anxious is part of being human, and Scripture treats worried people with tenderness rather than condemnation — think of Jesus' gentle "come to me… and I will give you rest" (Matthew 11:28). The Bible's encouragement is not to shame the feeling but to bring it to God rather than carry it alone.
