How to Start a Prayer Journal (Without Overcomplicating It)

A prayer journal turns scattered good intentions into a habit you can actually keep — and, over time, into a record of how God has answered. You don't need a special notebook or a system. Here's a simple way to start and stick with it.

Most of us have knelt to pray and, within a minute, realized we've forgotten half of what we meant to bring before God — and remembered three other worries instead. A prayer journal fixes that. Writing your prayers down keeps you focused while you pray, makes sure the people and needs on your heart don't slip through the cracks, and leaves a trail you can look back on to see God at work. Best of all, starting one is much simpler than it's often made to sound.

Why keep a prayer journal?

  • It keeps you focused. A written list holds your attention far better than trying to hold everything in your head, where the mind quickly wanders.
  • Nothing gets forgotten. The friend who asked for prayer, the decision you're facing, the person far from God — all of it stays in front of you.
  • You see God answer. This is the quiet joy of it. Looking back over months of requests and noting which have been answered builds real, evidence-based faith.
  • It slows you down. The act of writing turns a rushed prayer into unhurried time with God.

A simple way to start

The biggest reason prayer journals get abandoned is that people begin with a beautiful, elaborate system and can't sustain it. Start smaller than you think you need to. A single page and five minutes is plenty. If you'd like a gentle structure, the classic ACTS pattern is hard to beat — it keeps your prayers from becoming a list of only requests:

  • A — Adoration. Begin by praising God for who He is.
  • C — Confession. Honestly bring what you need to before Him.
  • T — Thanksgiving. Thank Him for specific things, large and small.
  • S — Supplication. Then bring your requests — for others and for yourself.

You don't have to use ACTS every day. Some days you'll only jot a few requests; other days you'll write a paragraph of thanks. The point isn't to fill a page — it's to show up.

Three formats that work

There's no single "right" layout. Pick whichever fits how you actually pray — or rotate between them:

  • A weekly prayer focus. Give each day of the week a theme — family on Monday, your church on Tuesday, missions on Wednesday, and so on. Over a week you cover far more ground than you could in one sitting, and no day feels overwhelming.
  • A request tracker. A simple table with three columns: the date, the request, and when it was answered. That last column is the heart of it — watching it fill in is a steady, tangible encouragement.
  • A journal page. Labelled sections for praise, requests, answered prayers, and a verse for the day. This suits a fuller, unhurried quiet time.

Many people keep two side by side: a weekly focus for their regular praying, and a running tracker for specific requests they want to follow to an answer.

What to actually write

If you sit down and draw a blank, here are the things worth capturing:

  • Requests, with dates. Note the date you first prayed for something — it makes answered prayers unmistakable later.
  • Answers. When a prayer is answered, write it down, even briefly. This is the record your future self will treasure.
  • Thanksgivings. Specific things you're grateful for keep the journal from becoming only a worry list.
  • A verse. A short passage that spoke to you, to pray back to God.
  • People by name. Family, friends, leaders, those who don't yet know Christ.

How to keep the habit going

A journal only helps if you open it, so treat the habit the way you'd treat any habit worth keeping:

  • Anchor it to a fixed time. Attach prayer to something you already do daily — your morning coffee, the commute, the last few minutes before bed. A consistent time beats a long time.
  • Keep it visible. A journal left open where you'll pray gets used; one in a drawer doesn't. Keep it with your Bible.
  • Lower the bar on hard days. Some days a single line is all you'll manage. Write the one line. Consistency matters far more than length, and a short entry keeps the streak alive.
  • Revisit your answered prayers. Reading back over answered requests is both encouraging and the best motivation to keep going.

Frequently asked questions

What do you write in a prayer journal?

Mainly your prayer requests with the date, answers when they come, things you're thankful for, a verse that spoke to you, and the people you're praying for by name. A simple structure like Adoration, Confession, Thanksgiving, Supplication (ACTS) can guide each entry.

How do I start a prayer journal as a beginner?

Start small — one page and five minutes a day. Write a few requests with today's date, one or two things you're thankful for, and leave room to note answers later. Don't aim to fill a page; aim to show up consistently.

What's the ACTS prayer method?

ACTS is a simple pattern for balanced prayer: Adoration (praising God), Confession, Thanksgiving, and Supplication (bringing your requests). It keeps a prayer journal from becoming only a list of things you want.

How do I keep up the habit of journaling my prayers?

Anchor it to an existing daily routine, keep the journal visible with your Bible, and allow yourself very short entries on busy days. Revisiting answered prayers is one of the best ways to stay motivated.
